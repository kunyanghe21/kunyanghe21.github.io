Point Process

One way of characterizing the statistical properties of a point process is to define a set of non-negative integer-valued random variables, \(N(A)\) , for each \(A \subset \mathcal{A}\) , such that \(N(A)\) is the number of points in the set \(A\) . Specifying in a consistent way the probability distribution of each of the \(N(A)\) determines the characteristics of the point process, which we label as \(N\) . Summary features of a point proress can also be defined. In particular,

\[\Lambda(A)=\mathrm{E}\{N(A)\}\]

which gives the expected number points in any subset \(A \subset \mathcal{A}\) , is defined to be the intensity measure of the process. Assuming \(A=\left[a_{1}, x_{1}\right] \times \cdots \times \left[a_{k}, x_{k}\right] \subset \mathbb{R}^{k}\) , and provided it exists, the derivative function

\[\lambda(\boldsymbol{x})=\frac{\partial \Lambda(A)}{\partial x_{1} \cdots \partial x_{k}}\]

is the intensity (density) function of the process. The canonical point process is the one-dimensional homogeneous Poisson process. With a parameter \(\lambda>0\) , this is a process on \(\mathcal{A} \subset \mathbb{R}\) satisfying: 1. for all \(A=\left[t_{1}, t_{2}\right] \subset \mathcal{A}\) ,

\[N(A) \sim \operatorname{Poi}\left(\lambda\left(t_{2}-t_{1}\right)\right)\]

  1. for all non-overlapping subsets \(A\) and \(B\) of \(\mathcal{A}\), \(N(A)\) and \(N(B)\) are independent random variables.

The homogeneous Poisson process can be generalized to a model for points that occur randomly in time, but at a variable rate \(\lambda(t)\) . This leads to the one-dimensional non-homogeneous Poisson process, which has the same property of independent counts on non-overlapping subsets as the homogeneous Poisson process, but the modified property that, for all \(A=\left[t_{1}, t_{2}\right] \subset \mathcal{A}\)

\[N(A) \sim \operatorname{Poi}(\Lambda(A))\] where

\[\Lambda(A)=\int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}} \lambda(t) d t\]

Implicitly, the intensity measure and density functions are \(\Lambda(\cdot) and \lambda(\cdot)\) respectively.

The non-homogeneous Poisson process generalizes further to provide a description of randomly occurring points in a subset of k -dimensional space. A point process on \(\mathcal{A} \subset \mathbb{R}^{k}\) is said to be a k -dimensional nonhomogeneous Poisson process, with intensity density function \(\lambda(\cdot)\) , if it satisfies the property of independent counts on non-overlapping subsets and, for all \(A \subset \mathcal{A}\) ,

\[N(A) \sim \operatorname{Poi}(\Lambda(A))\]

where

\[\Lambda(A)=\int_{A} \lambda(\boldsymbol{x}) d \boldsymbol{x}\] As usual, the likelihood is derived by regarding the probability of the observed data configuration as a function of the unknown parameter \(\theta\) . We give the development in the simplest case, where \(\mathcal{A}\) is one-dimensional, but the argument is similar for Poisson processes in higher dimensions.

So, suppose points \(x_{1}\) , \(\ldots, x_{n}\) have been observed in a region \(\mathcal{A} \subset \mathbb{R}\) , and that these are the realization of a Poisson process on \(\mathcal{A}\) : with intensity function \(\lambda(\cdot ; \theta)\) , for some value of \(\theta\) . The information contained in the data is that points have occurred at a number of known locations, but nowhere else in the region. The likelihood is derived using both these pieces of information. We let \(I_{i}=\left[x_{i}, x_{i}+\delta_{i}\right]\) , for \(i=1, \ldots, n\) , be a set of small intervals based around the observations, and write \(\mathcal{I}=\mathcal{A} \backslash \bigcup_{i=1}^{n} I_{i}\) . By the Poisson property,

\[\operatorname{Pr}\left\{N\left(I_{i}\right)=1\right\}=\exp \left\{-\Lambda\left(I_{i} ; \theta\right)\right\} \Lambda\left(I_{i} ; \theta\right)\]

where

\[\Lambda\left(I_{i} ; \theta\right)=\int_{x_{i}}^{x_{i}+\delta_{i}} \lambda(u) d u \approx \lambda\left(x_{i}\right) \delta_{i}\]

Substituting (7.2) in (7.1) gives

\[\operatorname{Pr}\left\{N\left(I_{i}\right)=1\right\} \approx \exp \left\{-\lambda\left(x_{i}\right) \delta_{i}\right\} \lambda\left(x_{i}\right) \delta_{i} \approx \lambda\left(x_{i}\right) \delta_{i}\]

where we have used the fact that \(\exp \left\{-\lambda\left(x_{i}\right) \delta_{i}\right\} \approx 1\) for small \(\delta_{i}\) . Also,

\[\operatorname{Pr}\{N(\mathcal{I})=0\}=\exp \{-\Lambda(\mathcal{I})\} \approx \exp \{-\Lambda(\mathcal{A})\}\]

since the \(\delta_{i}\) are all small. Hence, the likelihood is

\[\begin{aligned} L\left(\theta ; x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\right) &=\operatorname{Pr}\left\{N(\mathcal{I})=0, N\left(I_{1}\right)=1, N\left(I_{2}\right)=1, \ldots, N\left(I_{n}\right)=1\right\} \\ &=\operatorname{Pr}\{N(\mathcal{I})=0\} \prod_{i=1}^{n} \operatorname{Pr}\left\{N\left(I_{i}\right)=1\right\} \\ & \approx \exp \{-\Lambda(\mathcal{A} ; \theta)\} \prod_{i=1}^{n} \lambda\left(x_{i} ; \theta\right) \delta_{i} \end{aligned}\]

Dividing through by the \(\delta_{i}\) to obtain a density leads to

\[L\left(\theta ; x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\right)=\exp \{-\Lambda(\mathcal{A} ; \theta)\} \prod_{i=1}^{n} \lambda\left(x_{i} ; \theta\right)\]

where

\[\Lambda(\mathcal{A} ; \theta)=\int_{\mathcal{A}} \lambda(x ; \theta) d x\]

Likelihood (7.3) is also valid in the more general case of a Poisson process on a k -dimensional set \(\mathcal{A}\) .

The simplest application of (7.3) is to the situation where \(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\) are the points of a one-dimensional homogeneous Poisson process, with unknown intensity parameter \(\lambda\) , observed over an interval \(\mathcal{A}=[0, t]\) . In that case,

\[\Lambda(\mathcal{A} ; \lambda)=\lambda t\]

and \(\mathrm{SO}\)

\[L\left(\lambda ; x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\right)=\exp \{-\lambda t\} \lambda^{n}\]

As usual, it is easier to maximize the log-likelihood

\[\ell(\lambda)=\log L\left(\lambda ; x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\right)=-\lambda t+n \log \lambda\]

leading to the maximum likelihood estimator

\[\hat{\lambda}=n / t\]

which is the empirical rate of point occurrence. Maximization of (7.3) for non-homogeneous Poisson process models generally requires numerical techniques.

To exploit point processes as a representation for extreme values, we need a notion of convergence that is the analog of convergence of random variables.

Definition 7.1 Let \(N_{1}, N_{2}, \ldots\) be a sequence of point processes on \(\mathcal{A}\) . The sequence is said to converge in distribution to \(N\) , denoted

\[N_{n} \stackrel{d}{\rightarrow} N\] ,

if, for all choices of \(m\) and for all bounded sets \(A_{1}, \ldots, A_{m}\) such that \(\operatorname{Pr}\left\{N\left(\partial A_{j}\right)=0\right\}=1, j=1 \ldots, m\) , where \(\partial A\) is the boundary of \(A\) , the joint distribution of \(\left(N_{n}\left(A_{1}\right), \ldots, N_{n}\left(A_{m}\right)\right)\) converges in distribution to \(\left(N\left(A_{1}\right), \ldots, N_{n}\left(A_{m}\right)\right)\)

Less formally, \(N_{n} \stackrel{d}{\rightarrow} N\) if the probabilistic properties of \(N_{n}\) and \(N\) are arbitrarily similar for large enough \(n\) .

Convergence Law The point process framework provides an elegant way to formulate extreme value limit results. As in the threshold excess model formulation of Chapter 4 , we assume \(X_{1}, X_{2}, \ldots\) to be a series of independent and identically distributed random variables, with common distribution function \(F\) . We suppose that the \(X_{i}\) are well behaved in an extreme value sense. That is, with \(M_{n}=\max \left\{X_{1}, \ldots, X_{n}\right\}\) , that there are sequences of constants \(\left\{a_{n}>0\right\} and \left\{b_{n}\right\}\) such that

\[\operatorname{Pr}\left\{\left(M_{n}-b_{n}\right) / a_{n} \leq z\right\} \rightarrow G(z)\] with

\[G(z)=\exp \left\{-\left[1+\xi\left(\frac{z-\mu}{\sigma}\right)\right]^{-1 / \xi}\right\}\]

for some parameters \(\mu, \sigma>0\) and \(\xi\) . We then define a sequence of point processes \(N_{n}\) on \(\mathbb{R}^{2}\) by

\[N_{n}=\left\{\left(i /(n+1),\left(X_{i}-b_{n}\right) / a_{n}\right): i=1, \ldots, n\right\}\] .

The scaling in the first ordinate ensures that the time axis is always mapped to (0,1) ; the scaling in the second ordinate stabilizes the behavior of extremes as \(n \rightarrow \infty\) . The fundamental result, stated more precisely below in Theorem 7.1 , is that on regions of the form \([0,1] \times[u, \infty), N_{n} \stackrel{d}{\rightarrow} N\) as \(n \rightarrow \infty\) , where \(N\) is a non-homogeneous Poisson process. An outline of the argument follows.

Consider a region of the form \(A=[0,1] \times(u, \infty)\) for some large value of \(u\) . Then, each of the \(n\) points of \(N_{n}\) has probability \(p\) of falling in \(A\) , where

\[p=\operatorname{Pr}\left\{\left(X_{i}-b_{n}\right) / a_{n}>u\right\} \approx \frac{1}{n}\left[1+\xi\left(\frac{u-\mu}{\sigma}\right)\right]^{-1 / \xi}\]

by (4.7). Since the \(X_{i}\) are mutually independent, \(N_{n}(A)\) has the binomial distribution

\[N_{n}(A) \sim \operatorname{Bin}(n, p)\] .

By the standard convergence of a binomial distribution to a Poisson limit, combining (7.4) and (7.2), the limiting distribution of \(N_{n}(A)\) as \(n \rightarrow \infty\) is \(\operatorname{Poi}(\Lambda(A))\) , with

\[\Lambda(A)=\left[1+\xi\left(\frac{u-\mu}{\sigma}\right)\right]^{-1 / \xi}\]

Because of the homogeneity of the process in the time direction, it follows that, for any region of the form \(A=\left[t_{1}, t_{2}\right] \times(u, \infty)\) , with \(\left[t_{1}, t_{2}\right] \subset[0,1]\) , the limiting distribution of \(N_{n}(A)\) is also \(\operatorname{Poi}(\Lambda(A))\) , where

\[\Lambda(A)=\left(t_{2}-t_{1}\right)\left[1+\xi\left(\frac{u-\mu}{\sigma}\right)\right]^{-1 / \xi}\] .

This Poisson limit for all such sets \(A\) , together with the fact that the distributions of the N(A) on non-overlapping sets are bound to be independent by construction, is sufficient to establish the Poisson process limit, with intensity measure of the limiting process given by (7.5). The result is summarized in the following theorem.

Theorem 7.1 Let \(X_{1}, X_{2}, \ldots\) be a series of independent and identically distributed random variables for which there are sequences of constants \(\left\{a_{n}>0\right\}\) and \(\left\{b_{n}\right\}\) such that

\[\operatorname{Pr}\left\{\left(M_{n}-b_{n}\right) / a_{n} \leq z\right\} \rightarrow G(z)\] where

\[G(z)=\exp \left\{-\left[1+\xi\left(\frac{z-\mu}{\sigma}\right)\right]^{-1 / \xi}\right\}\]

and let \(z_{-}\) and \(z_{+}\) be the lower and upper endpoints of \(G\) respectively. Then, the sequence of point processes

\[N_{n}=\left\{\left(i /(n+1),\left(X_{i}-b_{n}\right) / a_{n}\right): i=1, \ldots, n\right\}\]

converges on regions of the form \((0,1) \times[u, \infty)\) , for any \(u>z_{-}\) , to a Poisson process, with intensity measure on \(A=\left[t_{1}, t_{2}\right] \times\left[z, z_{+}\right)\) given by

\(\Lambda(A)=\left(t_{2}-t_{1}\right)\left[1+\xi\left(\frac{z-\mu}{\sigma}\right)\right]^{-1 / \xi}\) .

The likelihood function can be written as:

\(L\left(\theta ;\left(x_{1}, y_{1}\right), \ldots,\left(x_{n}, y_{n}\right)\right)=\prod_{i=1}^{n} \psi\left(\theta ;\left(x_{i}, y_{i}\right)\right)\)

where \(\theta\) denotes the parameters of \(F\) and

\[\psi(\theta ;(x, y))=\left\{\begin{array}{ll} \left.\frac{\partial^{2} F}{\partial x \partial y}\right|_{(x, y)} & \text { if }(x, y) \in R_{1,1} \\ \left.\frac{\partial F}{\partial x}\right|_{\left(x, u_{y}\right)} & \text { if }(x, y) \in R_{1,0} \\ \left.\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}\right|_{\left(u_{x}, y\right)} & \text { if }(x, y) \in R_{0,1} \\ F\left(u_{x}, u_{y}\right) & \text { if }(x, y) \in R_{0,0} \end{array}\right.\]

\[\operatorname{Pr}\left\{M_{x, n}^{*} \leq x, M_{y, n}^{*} \leq y\right\} \rightarrow G(x, y)\] .
